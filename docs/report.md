## Background research ##

### Challenges in Learning Mathematics ###


### Definition of Challenge in Context ###


A definition of 'challenge' for this particular context will be determined by two pertinent defintions. By triangulating sources, a wider scope of interpretation can be implemented on the term, thus bolstering the defintion's practicality (Naidu-Valentine, 2024). Sweller (2011) implicitly defines challenge as the degree to which tasks demand a high cognitive load, in relation to the learners capacity of working memory. This definition of 'challenge' can be directly applied to maths - where performance outcomes are determined by working memory (Allen et al., 2020). Challenge can also be defined in reference to the probability of success at a given ability level; If the probability is low that task completion is successful, then the task can be referred to as challenging (Lord, 1980). With this definition of challenge, describing maths as challenging is completely valid when comparing large sets of exam results (Coe et al., 2008). Using both defintions for the term challenge/challenging allows the term to encompass a broad range of interpretations which strengthen the validity of usage.


### Perception of Mathematics Difficulty ###
//MAY NEED TO DISCUSS PROS OF CUFF2017 //EDIT
In the context of school, It is generally assumed by society that maths is a comparitively challenging subject when compared to curricula (Cuff, 2017). This assumption is reasonably founded and reflects research conclusions (Coe et al., 2008). The study analysed ten's of thousand's of exam results accross a 12 year period and produced findings which show maths and STEM generally receiving the lowest levels of attainment among students of similar prior ability, in comparison to the over curricula. This indicates that maths is more challening than most other subjects unrelated to STEM. There is some research which suggests that the degree to which a subject is challenging is largely unrelated to content, but rather determined by frequency of engagement and teacher/student relationships - “specific subject area did not seem to matter much for the degree to which students experienced a subject as difficult or easy” (Roorda et al., 2019). The data was collected via self reporting which whilst effective for pooling together large datasets, suffers from the posibility of response bias which substantially reduces the validity of claims (Adams et al., 1999). More research would need to be performed in order to reduce potential biases in self reporting studies.


## Game Based Learning in Education ##

### Games as Medium ###
//A BIT SLOPPY, NEEDS SOME TWEAK
The utilisation of games as the medium through which learning takes place has been unequivicolly shown to: bolster motivation, increase engagement, improve attitudes, enhance enjoyment, and inspire learning. through large meta analysis that 84% of 57 research journals reported positive results from implementing a game through which to provide maths education(Vankúš, 2021). This finding has been replicated in another large meta-analysis where thorugh 43 studies, it was found that games enhance the sense of achievment during the learning process (Pan et al., 2022), which is known to reduce challenge amongst learners (Moroshkina et al., 2024). There was also evidence which suggests that mechanics can obscure the link between gameplay and learning - hindering the learning process (Pan et al., 2022). This leaves want for determining exactly what kinds of games are best suited to maths learning. Games are fundamentally sets of coordinating mechanics, therefore in order for the education element to be succesfully integrated into the game, it has to be congruent with the mechanics.


### RPG Mechanics and Learning ###
//NEEDS ADJUSTING AT THE END
RPG’s are centered on the idea that player choices will alter the outcome of: character development, narrative, and progression (Adams, 2014). Player choices are often central to an RPG's core mechanics often being directly baked into the gameplay via dialogue options, player stats, selected items, and destination choice. Player choices are often contained within a single instance of gameplay - referred to as an event - where each option acts as a controller tweaking underlying mechanics, increasing/decreasing the likelihood of outcomes (Adams, 2014). The probabilistic element of gameplay can be strengthend by including the use of die at every event, producing drastic implications on the variety of outcomes available to the player. By displaying the probability/statistical information of player choice at every event in a visually appealing manner, the array of outcomes that are available become unobscured and the player can engage with the choices in a more calculated and nuanced way. Furthermore, player choice becomes pertinent due to the clarity of potential upsides/downsides, increasing the likelihood of meaningful attachment and engagement from the player (Salen & Zimmerman, 2004). These mechanics therefore, are accurate representatations of game design well suited to learning/teaching(Vankúš, 2021). Since these graphical displays are wired into gameplay at every event, the link between mechanics and learning is strong and the two areas cohere together (Pan et al., 2022). Another important factor relating to the graphic involves repeated exposure. Game‑based repeated exposure can improve retention of maths concepts(Mosia & Egara, 2024). Furthermore, the interpretation of aforementioned graphic displays would be a highly personal and variable endeavour; No two players would make the same choices. This creates a highly personalised RPG experience which reduces cognitive load(Zhong, 2022) and thus decreases the degree of challenge inherent to the mathematical concepts being displayed (Sweller, 2011). The evidence suggests that the fundamental mechanic - displaying probabilities underpinning player choice events - of the proposed project would be supportive of learners.

### Prodigy Math: Kids game ###


Prodigy Math is a charming web based RPG/Adventure game, stylistically catered to a younger audience. It’s features include many of the typical attributes associated with RPG game: A level system, turn based combat system, designable characters, base player stats. It delivers education through the combat system - if the player runs out of magic, they must complete maths questions to replenish their magic, and thus continue the fight. If they answer correctly, magic acquired, if not, they miss a turn. It is a simple method of introducing teaching mechanics to the game, and has been used in previous releases(source).

DragonBox is an award winning series of e-learning apps aimed at children and students aged 5-15. The focus of DragonBox is to teach algebra through a clever board-game arrangement, which requires the players to form and complete algebraic equations, but without the algebra being explicitly displayed. As the game progresses, cute icons which are used instead of the numerator/operators transition into numbers and variables. The game is renowned for its intuitive mechanics which accommodate learning, winning awards in 2017 including a gold from International Serious Play Awards and Best Learning Game from Games for Change Awards (Alhadeff, 2017; Martindale, 2017).
